01 · Overview

Project Abstract & Broad Objectives

EcoRoute: AI Carbon Leak Detector is a smart, AI-powered system designed to detect, monitor, and minimise carbon emissions from vehicles and logistics operations in real time. As global concern over climate change grows, the need for intelligent, scalable solutions that can address vehicular carbon footprint has never been more urgent. This project combines the power of Artificial Intelligence, sensor-based data collection, and route optimisation algorithms to identify high-emission "leaks" in transportation networks and suggest eco-friendly alternatives. The system aims to serve urban planners, fleet managers, and individual users alike, bridging the gap between cutting-edge AI research and practical, real-world sustainability solutions.

  • Develop an AI-based system capable of monitoring and detecting CO₂ and other vehicular emissions in real time using sensor data and machine learning models.
  • Implement intelligent eco-routing algorithms that suggest optimised, low-emission paths for vehicles, reducing the overall carbon footprint of journeys.
  • Build a scalable and accessible monitoring dashboard that visualises emission levels, route comparisons, and historical trends for decision-making.
  • Identify and flag "carbon leak" hotspots — specific routes, intersections, or behaviours that contribute disproportionately to emissions.
  • Evaluate the system's effectiveness in reducing predicted carbon emissions against baseline models and prior literature benchmarks.

02 · Problem Statement

Understanding the Problem

General Description

Transportation is one of the largest contributors to global greenhouse gas emissions. Despite the availability of navigation apps, most route planning tools optimise only for time or distance — completely ignoring the carbon cost of each route. Vehicles idling at congestion hotspots, inefficient routing, and lack of real-time emission feedback create invisible "carbon leaks" in our everyday mobility. There exists a pressing need for a system that can intelligently detect, quantify, and reduce these leaks using AI and real-time data.

Importance & Context

  • Road transport accounts for nearly 16% of global CO₂ emissions
  • Most existing solutions are either hardware-heavy or limited to offline analysis
  • AI-driven eco-routing can reduce emissions by 10–20% on average
  • Smart cities and sustainable logistics demand real-time carbon intelligence
  • No unified platform currently integrates detection + routing + visualisation
Our Reflection

Our interest in this problem was sparked during a discussion about how map applications like Google Maps show traffic but never carbon impact. We began to wonder — what if a navigation system could think ecologically? As we discussed this with our guide Dr. Abdul Lateef Haroon P S , our framing evolved from a vague "green routing idea" to a focused challenge: building a system that detects where emissions are highest and intelligently reroutes traffic. Inputs from the PRC helped us understand the importance of quantifiability — the system must not just sense, but measure and act.

03 · Objectives

Refined Project Objectives

Our objectives were refined iteratively across multiple sessions with our project guide and PRC committee. What began as broad sustainability goals were sharpened into the following measurable, technically grounded objectives:

  • Design and simulate a real-time CO₂ emission monitoring module using IoT sensor data or equivalent software-based proxies, integrated with vehicle speed and location data.
  • Implement and compare AI/ML models — including regression, classification, and reinforcement learning approaches — to predict emission levels and recommend optimal routes.
  • Develop an eco-routing engine capable of dynamically adjusting suggested paths based on live traffic and emission predictions.
  • Create a user-facing dashboard or interface to visualise emission data, route comparisons, and carbon savings in an intuitive way.
  • Validate the system performance using benchmark datasets and compare against existing eco-routing literature.
How Objectives Evolved

Initially, our objectives were broad — "reduce carbon emissions using AI." Through guidance from Dr. Abdul Lateef Haroon P S, we learned to frame measurable goals. The PRC review pushed us to distinguish between monitoring (detecting leaks) and acting (rerouting), which led us to split our objective into detection and routing sub-goals. We also moved from a purely hardware approach to a hybrid software-simulation approach, making the project more feasible within our timeline.

Our Reflection on the Literature

Reviewing this body of research revealed a consistent theme: existing solutions are either hardware-intensive (IoT sensor networks) or narrowly focused (AI energy tracking without routing). A clear trend emerged toward AI-based route optimisation, but no single study unifies real-time emission detection with intelligent eco-routing in a lightweight, scalable architecture. The gap that stood out most was the absence of a platform that bridges the "detect" and "redirect" phases of carbon management — which directly informed our proposed solution. We also noticed that most studies were validated in developed-world contexts, ignoring constraints in developing or semi-urban environments.

05 · Research Gap Identification

Where Existing Work Falls Short

🔌 Hardware Dependency

Most emission monitoring systems require dedicated physical IoT hardware, limiting deployment scalability and accessibility in resource-constrained environments.

🔄 Disconnected Pipelines

Detection and rerouting are treated as separate problems. No unified system currently integrates carbon leak identification with real-time eco-routing in a single loop.

📊 Lack of Real-Time Intelligence

Many approaches rely on offline batch analysis or historical datasets. A gap exists for systems that adapt dynamically to live traffic and emission data simultaneously.

🌍 Context Gaps

Solutions are validated primarily in developed urban contexts. Semi-urban and developing-region road networks — with irregular traffic behaviour — remain largely unaddressed.

👤 User Accessibility

Existing tools are oriented toward researchers and fleet operators. A gap exists for lightweight, user-friendly interfaces that empower individual drivers to make eco-conscious decisions.

🤖 AI Model Scope

AI in prior work is applied either to emission tracking or to route optimisation — rarely both. A combined ML framework addressing the full carbon-detection-to-action pipeline is missing.

Gap vs. Limitation — Our Understanding

A key insight from our literature review was learning to differentiate a "gap" from a "limitation." A limitation is a constraint inherent to a specific study (e.g., "requires high computational resources"), while a gap is an unaddressed need in the broader research landscape. For instance, the fact that no existing system integrates real-time detection with rerouting in a single lightweight platform is a gap — not tied to any one paper's limitations, but an opportunity in the field as a whole. This distinction helped us articulate where our contribution genuinely sits.

06 · Proposed Solution

Conceptual Framework (Black-Box View)

EcoRoute System — Black Box

📡 Input: Vehicle Data, GPS, Traffic Feeds
↓
🧠 AI Emission Detection Engine
↓
🗺️ Eco-Routing Optimisation Module
↓
📊 Carbon Leak Dashboard & Alerts
↓
✅ Output: Optimised Route + Emission Report

High-Level Explanation

The EcoRoute system takes in real-time or simulated vehicle and traffic data, processes it through an AI model to quantify emissions, identifies "carbon leak" zones, and outputs an eco-optimised route recommendation. The system operates as a closed loop — continuously learning and adjusting as new data flows in.

What Influenced Our Idea

The concept emerged from combining two observations: (1) navigation apps know where traffic is but not its carbon cost, and (2) AI models in logistics can optimise for fuel but rarely for real-time CO₂. Our guide encouraged us to think in terms of systems integration, which shaped our black-box architecture.

Alternatives We Considered & Rejected

We initially considered a purely hardware-based solution using physical CO₂ sensors mounted on vehicles. While accurate, this approach was cost-prohibitive and difficult to scale. We also considered a mobile app-only solution, but this lacked the processing capacity for real-time ML inference. Our final approach balances software simulation with AI inference, keeping the system deployable without specialised hardware — a direction reinforced through discussion with Dr. Abdul Lateef Haroon P S.

07 · Methodology

Conceptual Approach — High-Level Steps

1

Data Collection & Preprocessing

Gather vehicle emission datasets, GPS trajectory data, and traffic flow information from public repositories and simulation tools. Clean, normalise, and structure the data for ML model training.

2

Emission Prediction Modelling

Train and evaluate AI/ML models (regression, neural networks) to predict CO₂ emissions based on vehicle speed, route type, and traffic density. Identify high-emission zones algorithmically.

3

Eco-Routing Algorithm Development

Develop or adapt a routing algorithm (e.g., A*, Dijkstra with emission weights, or reinforcement learning) to compute the lowest-emission path between any two points given real-time constraints.

4

System Integration & Dashboard

Integrate emission detection and routing modules into a unified pipeline. Build a visualisation dashboard to display routes, emission comparisons, and carbon savings for the end user.

5

Testing, Evaluation & Benchmarking

Test the system against benchmark datasets and compare results with existing methods from literature. Measure emission reduction percentages and routing accuracy as key performance indicators.

Challenges & Mentor Involvement

Structuring the methodology was initially difficult because we were unsure how to sequence the detection and routing components — were they parallel or sequential? Dr. Abdul Lateef Haroon P S helped us understand that detection must precede routing in each cycle, making the pipeline sequential. The PRC also highlighted that we needed to define our evaluation metrics upfront, rather than treating them as an afterthought. This guidance was crucial in giving our methodology a clear, defensible structure.
